The First Internet

An Academic Network

ARPANet was developed by the Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA), it was a network that started out as an interconnection of four university computers--the University of California, the University of California Santa Barbara, the University of Utah and the Massachussets Institute of Techniology and Stanford Research Institute. The first message sent through this network of networks was from UCLA to SRI on October 29, 1969, logging in. As the connection failed mid-message, the only letters transmitted were L and O. Before this development, computers weren't considered as communication devices. It was until J. C. R. Licklider proposed a study, that it would soon become a reality.

This began the journey of ARPANet from being a military construct to one for research and academe as more and more universities and research institutions were added to the network across the coasts of the United States. In 1971, Ray Tomlinson from Bolt Beranek and Newman (BBN) sent the first official intercomputer e-mail.

Eventually, they were able to expand across oceans and establish a node in 1973 with the University of London and expand interconnectivity. This was made possible by satellite (SATNET). By 1977, there were 111 nodes on the ARPANET (www.livinginternet.com). Each node had an Interface Message Processor (IMP), a pre-decessor of today's Routers, used to interconnect participant networks to the ARPANET.

ARPANet expanded the horizons of possibilities and understanding. Other networks soon emerged, like Computer Scence Network (CSNet), Military Network (MILNET), and the National Science Foundation Network. The internet was quickly beginning to form as more and more people ventured and invested into internetwork connectivity. The NSFNet backbone was quickly changing the landscape of the soon-to-be internet. With the emergence of other networks, the usage of ARPANet eventually declined and shut down.

We go on further towards present day as the internet develops and expands beyond the academe.
