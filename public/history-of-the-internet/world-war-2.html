World War 2

The Sputnik Crisis and The Dawn of the Internet

Some fifty years ago, before the internet and the events leading up to it's establishment, communication and interaction was very much a linear process. Mail was sent by post from a sender to a receiver, passing through the hands of sorters and messengers--which don't differ much from the protocols inside the computer--at a much slower pace. Taking a few hours to a few weeks to send and receive messages. The internet made it possible to send data faster.

Among others, telephone and radio were other popular means to communication at the time--this also includes military. Efforts were made, of course, to ensure that the connection was secure and any unwanted eavesdroppers could not intercept their messages.

This was significant in the wake of World War II and the dawn of the Cold War, because, with the political and social climate of the early 1900's, a need was created for secure and private communications. And with the launch of Sputnik I in 1957 by Soviet Russia, the United States was given a jolt. The sudden success of the Soviet Sputnik program made evident a technological gap between the two super powers.

Telephone and radio communication--which was very much still the only means of communication at the time--was made possible by huge towers that transmit radio waves to the receiving end. Electromagnetic waves that--in the presence of an electromagnetic pulse--are disrupted. In the event of a nuclear fallout, which was very much a fear following the final days of World War II in 1945, the massive electromagnetic pulse would render radio communication impossible. The military would be left blind.

Amidst tensions rising between international superpowers, a research and development project was launched, Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA). For a time, DARPA focused on space and strategic missile research but later set aside these projects to focus on computing and information processing.

This eventually led to the creation of ARPANET, a network that started out as an interconnection of four university computers but became the basis for the internet.
