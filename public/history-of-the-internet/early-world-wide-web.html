Early World Wide Web

The New Land of Opportunity

As the computer science world bid goodbye to the ARPANET and eventually NSFNet, the World Wide Web opened the internet's doors to the general public. Created by Tim Berners-Lee, a computer scientist at Conseil Européen pour la Recherche Nucléaire (European Organization for Nuclear Research, CERN), the World Wide Web is what we more commonly refer to as the internet. This changed the way information was accessed. His proposal for information management made it so that information could be accessed by hyperlinks. The World Wide Web made the internet more accessible to the general public by revamping the way information was accessed. This eventually fostered the creation of an interface that translated computer data into text and images through the web browser.

If the first transmitted message via the internet was supposed to be LOGIN, then the World Wide Web's first website was info.CERN.ch. Amusing, is the fact that if you had accidentally shut down Tim Berners-Lee's computer, you would have shut down the internet. That much power is bound to drive you just a bit zany. In a video from VSauce, Michael acknowledges the abundance of ocean and aquatic terminologies attributed or analogized to the World Wide Web. Goes to show you just how much of a mind-blowing concept the internet is today. Personally, I have a bias towards the ocean and the analogies made of it.

In this period, as the twentieth century came to a close, the internet was just showing us the tip of the iceberg of what we could accomplish. As we ushered in the new millenium with uncertainty and exploration, we usher in the Information Age and the era of modern internet.
